Ethiopia

Neocolonialism

China plays a significant role in Ethiopia, having provided the country with approximately €12 billion in loans. These funds are mainly used to develop infrastructure, including tram systems, railway lines, and industrial parks. Ethiopia views this as an opportunity for economic growth and job creation.

The Chinese loans are part of the Belt and Road Initiative, which aims to expand trade routes and create economic dependencies. Critics warn of a potential "debt trap," as Ethiopia's national debt has already reached 59% of its GDP. In response, China extended repayment periods for certain loans.

Ethiopia closely follows China’s economic model, establishing special economic zones to drive industrialization. The government hopes to replicate China’s success in creating a middle class through industrial development. However, Ethiopia remains largely an agricultural economy.

China benefits from this partnership by securing economic and geopolitical influence. In Africa, however, Chinese involvement is controversial. The U.S. and the EU are trying to counterbalance China's presence with their own investments. Despite accusations of exploitation, Chinese firms have created jobs, and many Ethiopians view the investments positively. Prime Minister Abiy Ahmed is navigating between China’s economic influence and Western interests.

Colonial History

Ethiopia's history in the 19th and 20th centuries was marked by colonialism, modernization, and political upheaval. Emperor Tewodros II resisted European colonialism but clashed with Britain, leading to the British Ethiopian expedition in 1868. His successor, Yohannes IV, stabilized the empire and fought the Mahdists but was killed in battle in 1889.

Menelik II (1889–1913) expanded Ethiopia’s territory and successfully defended the country against Italian colonization. In 1896, Ethiopia defeated Italy in the Battle of Adwa, preserving its independence. Italy only managed to conquer Ethiopia under Mussolini in 1936, but British forces liberated the country in 1941.

Haile Selassie attempted modernization but remained authoritarian. In 1974, he was overthrown by a socialist military dictatorship (Derg) led by Mengistu Haile Mariam. The Derg regime brought repression, famine, and civil wars. In 1991, the regime collapsed, Eritrea gained independence, and Ethiopia began democratization under the EPRDF.

Since then, Ethiopia has experienced economic growth but continues to face social and political challenges.
