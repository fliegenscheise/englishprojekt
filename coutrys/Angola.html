Angola

Neocolonialism

Kilamba, a city built by China in Angola, was initially designed as a modern housing project. However, the high prices made it unaffordable, leaving the city empty for years. Only after the government reduced prices and allowed payment plans over 30 years did people start moving in.

Today, Kilamba is home to over 100,000 residents. It features wide streets, parks, and modern apartment buildings, representing an urban revolution. However, serious structural issues—cracks in buildings, water damage, and poor construction quality—make the city look much older than it is. Despite these flaws, many residents feel safer and more comfortable compared to the slums of Luanda.

Angolan architects like Lidiu Daio believe the future lies in smaller, more sustainable houses with urban gardens to ensure food security. With China no longer dominating infrastructure projects, local companies are exploring innovations like 3D-printed homes.

While Kilamba offers many benefits, its poor construction quality raises concerns about whether it will still be livable in ten years.

Colonial History

Treatment of the Indigenous Population Under Portuguese Rule in Angola

Portuguese colonial rule in Angola was marked by economic exploitation, social discrimination, and brutal repression of the indigenous population. Enslavement, forced labor, and systemic oppression defined the lives of Angolans for centuries.

1. Enslavement and Forced Labor

From the moment the Portuguese arrived in 1483, the local population was used as labor—mostly under extreme coercion. During the peak of the transatlantic slave trade (16th–19th centuries), millions of Angolans were captured and sold to Brazil, the Caribbean, and Portugal. African intermediaries, including local rulers and traders, often played a role in this trade.

Even after the transatlantic slave trade was officially abolished in 1836, forced labor continued under different systems. One of the most notorious was "Chibalo", a system that forced African men to work unpaid for Portuguese businesses, plantations, and infrastructure projects. Those who refused faced harsh punishments or imprisonment.

2. Social Discrimination and Oppression

The Portuguese established a rigid social hierarchy in which white settlers and European immigrants held the highest positions, while the indigenous population was systematically marginalized.

Africans had little access to education, political rights, or economic opportunities. Some individuals could attain Portuguese citizenship through the "Assimilado" system, but only if they adopted European customs, spoke Portuguese, and converted to Catholicism. However, the criteria were so strict that only a small number of Africans were granted this status, while the majority remained second-class citizens.

3. Brutal Repression and Resistance

Any resistance to Portuguese rule was met with extreme violence. During the independence struggle of the 1960s and 1970s, the Portuguese military launched brutal counterinsurgency campaigns, destroying villages and using torture to suppress nationalist movements.

Despite this repression, Angolan resistance movements like MPLA, FNLA, and UNITA fought for independence. The overthrow of Portugal’s dictatorship in the 1974 Carnation Revolution led to the collapse of Portuguese colonial rule, and Angola finally gained independence on November 11, 1975.

Conclusion

Portuguese rule in Angola was characterized by severe exploitation and oppression of the indigenous population. Enslavement, forced labor, and social discrimination defined colonial Angola, and resistance was met with brutal repression. However, through decades of struggle, Angolans eventually won their independence.
